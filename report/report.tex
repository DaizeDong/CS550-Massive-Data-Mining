\section{Overview}
This project implements seven recommender models on the MovieLens-1M dataset and compares them on two tasks: rating prediction and Top-10 recommendation. The seven models cover four types of methods: simple baselines (\textsc{GlobalMean}, \textsc{BiasOnly}), memory-based collaborative filtering (\textsc{UserKNN}, \textsc{ItemKNN}), matrix factorization (\textsc{MF}, \textsc{BPR-MF}), neural methods (\textsc{NeuMF}), and graph-based method (\textsc{LightGCN}). All models are implemented from scratch using NumPy and PyTorch, without importing any recommender library.

%-----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Dataset}
MovieLens-1M contains $1{,}000{,}209$ integer ratings on a 1--5 scale from $6{,}040$ users on $3{,}706$ movies. Each user has at least 20 ratings. We drop items with fewer than 5 ratings to make similarity estimation more stable, leaving $N_u{=}6040$ users and $N_i{=}3416$ items.

The split follows the course spec: for each user, 20\% of ratings go to the test set (randomly chosen with seed 42). This gives $|\mathcal{D}_{\text{train}}|{=}799{,}721$ and $|\mathcal{D}_{\text{test}}|{=}199{,}890$, with 95.16\% sparsity.

\section{Methods}
Notation: user $u$, item $i$, observed rating $r_{ui}$, global mean $\mu$, user/item biases $b_u, b_i$, user and item latent factors $p_u, q_i \in \mathbb{R}^d$.

\subsection{Baselines}
\textbf{GlobalMean} predicts $\hat{r}_{ui} = \mu$ for all pairs. \textbf{BiasOnly} predicts $\hat{r}_{ui} = \mu + b_u + b_i$, where biases are learned by 15 alternating closed-form ridge updates with $\lambda = 5$.

\subsection{Neighbourhood CF}
We use shrinkage-adjusted cosine similarity on mean-centred ratings. For the item side:
\begin{equation}
s_{ij} = \frac{\sum_{u \in \mathcal{U}_{ij}} \tilde{r}_{ui} \tilde{r}_{uj}}{\|\tilde{r}_{\cdot i}\| \|\tilde{r}_{\cdot j}\|} \cdot \frac{|\mathcal{U}_{ij}|}{|\mathcal{U}_{ij}| + \alpha}, \qquad \tilde{r}_{ui} = r_{ui} - \bar{r}_u,\ \alpha = 100.
\end{equation}
We keep the top $k = 50$ neighbours per item. The prediction is:
\begin{equation}
\hat{r}_{ui} = \bar{r}_u + \frac{\sum_{j \in N_k(i) \cap \mathcal{I}_u} s_{ij} \tilde{r}_{uj}}{\sum_{j \in N_k(i) \cap \mathcal{I}_u} |s_{ij}|}.
\end{equation}
\textsc{UserKNN} works the same way but on the user side.

\subsection{Biased Matrix Factorization (MF)}
The prediction is $\hat{r}_{ui} = \mu + b_u + b_i + p_u^\top q_i$, trained with L2-regularized MSE loss. We use $d = 64$, 20 epochs of Adam, and $\lambda = 10^{-4}$.

\subsection{Neural Collaborative Filtering (NeuMF)}
NeuMF has two branches: a GMF branch computing $p_u^g \odot q_i^g$, and an MLP branch on the concatenation $[p_u^m \| q_i^m]$ with hidden sizes $(64, 32, 16)$. The outputs of both branches are concatenated and passed through a linear layer. The original NeuMF uses binary cross-entropy for implicit feedback; we use MSE instead since this task involves explicit ratings.

\subsection{BPR-MF}
BPR-MF trains on triples $(u, i, j)$ where $i$ is an observed item and $j$ is a randomly sampled unobserved item. The loss is:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{BPR}} = -\sum_{(u,i,j)} \log \sigma(\hat{x}_{ui} - \hat{x}_{uj}) + \lambda \|\Theta\|^2, \qquad \hat{x}_{ui} = p_u^\top q_i + b_i.
\end{equation}
Negative items are sampled uniformly with rejection against the positive set. We use $d = 64$, 20 epochs, Adam with learning rate $10^{-2}$.

\subsection{LightGCN}
LightGCN removes feature transforms and non-linearities from standard GCN, keeping only weighted-average propagation on the user-item bipartite graph. Let $\mathbf{A}$ be the adjacency matrix and $\hat{\mathbf{A}} = \mathbf{D}^{-1/2} \mathbf{A} \mathbf{D}^{-1/2}$ be its symmetric-normalized form. Each layer computes $\mathbf{E}^{(\ell)} = \hat{\mathbf{A}} \mathbf{E}^{(\ell-1)}$, and the final embedding averages over all layers:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{E}^{*} = \frac{1}{L+1} \sum_{\ell=0}^{L} \mathbf{E}^{(\ell)}.
\end{equation}
Training uses the same BPR loss. We set $L = 3$ and $d = 64$. We run LightGCN twice: once at 15 epochs with batch size 16,384, and once at 20 epochs with batch size 4,096 (matching the gradient-step count of BPR-MF).

%-----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Evaluation Protocol}
\textbf{Rating prediction}: MAE $= \frac{1}{|\mathcal{D}|}\sum |r - \hat{r}|$ and RMSE $= \sqrt{\frac{1}{|\mathcal{D}|}\sum (r - \hat{r})^2}$, with predictions clipped to $[1, 5]$.

\textbf{Top-$N$ recommendation}: for each user, we score all items, remove items already in the training set, take the top $K = 10$, and compute Precision@K, Recall@K, F$_1$@K, and NDCG@K, averaged over all users who have at least one test item.

BPR-MF and LightGCN produce scores that are not on the 1--5 rating scale, so their MAE and RMSE are marked as N/A.

\subsection{BPR-MF vs. MF: Loss Function Matters}
BPR-MF and MF use the same scoring function $p_u^\top q_i + b_i$, but different loss functions. BPR-MF beats MF by $2.55\times$ on P@10 ($0.286$ vs. $0.112$) and $2.57\times$ on NDCG@10 ($0.320$ vs. $0.125$). The reason is that MSE training tries to fit every rating value (including 2s and 3s), which is not directly useful for ranking. BPR training focuses on the relative order between observed and unobserved items, which is exactly what ranking metrics measure.

\subsection{kNN: Good at Rating, Bad at Ranking}
UserKNN and ItemKNN have competitive MAE (0.707 and 0.714 vs. MF's 0.704), but they perform poorly on ranking. This is because for items a user has not rated, the kNN prediction tends to fall back toward the user's mean rating. The neighbours that drive the prediction are mostly items the user has already rated, and those are masked out in Top-$N$ evaluation. So the predicted scores for unseen items do not vary enough to produce a good ranking.

\subsection{LightGCN vs. BPR-MF}
LightGCN performs worse than BPR-MF on all ranking metrics (P@10: 0.183 vs. 0.286, NDCG@10: 0.204 vs. 0.320). The first run used 15 epochs at batch size 16,384 (about 720 gradient steps), which is much fewer than BPR-MF's roughly 3,900 steps. To rule out the possibility that LightGCN was simply undertrained, we ran a second experiment with matched gradient steps (20 epochs, batch size 4,096). The results did not change at all (Table~\ref{tab:lgcnmatch}).

\input{lightgcn_match_table}

The gap is not caused by insufficient training. The likely reasons are:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
\item We used $L = 3$ layers, but $L = 2$ may work better on ML-1M. We did not sweep this hyperparameter.
\item Although the overall sparsity is 95.16\%, every user has at least 20 ratings, so a plain factor model already has enough direct signal per user. The extra neighbour-of-neighbour information from graph convolution may not add much in this setting.
\item Our regularization ($\lambda = 10^{-4}$, Adam defaults) was not tuned. The original paper's results come from a grid search.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{NeuMF}
NeuMF is slightly worse than MF on ranking (NDCG@10: 0.098 vs. 0.125) and about the same on rating prediction (MAE: 0.704 vs. 0.704). The extra neural layers do not improve results on this dataset.

\section{Exposure Fairness Analysis}\label{sec:fair}
Beyond accuracy, we also check whether the models concentrate all recommendations on popular items. For each model at $K = 10$, we measure:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Coverage}: fraction of items that appear in at least one user's Top-10 list.
\item \textbf{Gini}: inequality of per-item exposure counts. 0 means uniform, 1 means all exposure goes to one item.
\item \textbf{Long-tail Share}: fraction of recommendation slots that go to items outside the top 20\% most popular items in training.
\end{itemize}

\input{fairness_table}

LightGCN puts every Top-10 slot on popular items (Long-tail Share = 0.000) and only covers 2.2\% of the catalogue. BPR-MF is similar (Long-tail Share = 0.005, Coverage = 20.4\%). The two best-performing models on ranking accuracy are the two worst on fairness. On the other hand, UserKNN has 87\% of its list in the tail and covers 76\% of the catalogue, but its ranking accuracy is much lower.

Note that GlobalMean predicts the same score for all items, so the top-10 list is essentially random. Its high long-tail share is noise, not useful recommendation.

From these results, the models with higher ranking accuracy also concentrate recommendations more heavily on popular items.

\section{Demo}
\texttt{demo/app.py} is a Flask web app. The user picks a user ID and a model, and sees the top-10 recommended items alongside that user's highest-rated training items. All seven models are loaded from pickle files at startup, and per-user inference takes a few milliseconds.
